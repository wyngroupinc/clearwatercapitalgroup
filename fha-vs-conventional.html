FHA vs. Conventional: A Plain-Language Comparison

Two of the most common mortgage paths, how they differ, and where each one may make sense.

Clearwater Capital Group · 6 min read

FHA and conventional are two of the most widely used mortgage paths, and borrowers often wonder which one is "better." The honest answer is that it depends on your situation — they're built for different needs. Here's a plain-language look at how they compare.

Conventional loans, briefly

Conventional loans are not backed by a government agency. They're a common choice for borrowers with a solid credit profile and the ability to make a down payment. A few characteristics borrowers tend to value:

  • Flexibility on property types and terms.
  • Private mortgage insurance (PMI) may apply if the down payment is below a certain level — but it can often be removed later once equity conditions are met.
  • Pricing and qualification lean heavily on your overall credit and financial profile.

FHA loans, briefly

FHA loans are insured by the Federal Housing Administration and are often associated with helping buyers who have a smaller down payment or a developing credit profile. Key traits:

  • May allow a lower down payment than some conventional scenarios.
  • Can be more flexible on credit for some borrowers.
  • Mortgage insurance requirements apply, and they work differently from conventional PMI.
There's no universal winner. The "right" program is the one that fits your down payment, your credit profile, the property, and your longer-term plans.

How to think about the choice

Rather than starting with the program, start with your situation. How much do you have for a down payment? How does your credit look right now? How long do you expect to keep the loan? Those answers point toward the path that may fit. A borrower with a strong profile and a larger down payment might lean one way; a buyer prioritizing a lower upfront cost might lean another.

The value of comparing

This is where working with a brokerage helps: instead of being offered a single product, you can see how different programs stack up against each other for your specific numbers. The goal isn't to talk you into a program — it's to help you understand the trade-offs so the decision is yours.

Eligibility and requirements for both paths vary and are subject to qualification and underwriting, so the best next step is a conversation that looks at your actual profile.
